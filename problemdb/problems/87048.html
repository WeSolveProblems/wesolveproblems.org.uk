Problem ID 87048

Three non-coplanar vectors are given. Is it possible to find a fourth vector perpendicular to the three vectors given?

Hint

Apply the theory of the scalar product of vectors.

Solution

Suppose that there exists a nonzero vector $\overline{d}$ perpendicular to each of the three non-coplanar vectors $\overline{a}$, $\overline{b}$ and $\overline{c}$. Since the vectors $\overline{a}$, $\overline{b}$ and $\overline{c}$ are not coplanar, the numbers x, y and z exist such that

$\overline{d}$ = $x \times $\overline{a}$ + $y \times $\overline{b}$ + $z \times $\overline{c}$,

so

$\overline{d}^2$ = $\overline{d}$ \times $\overline{d}$ = $\overline{d}$ \times $($x \times $\overline{a}$ + $y \times $\overline{b}$ + $z \times $\overline{c}$)$ = $x \times $\overline{d}$ \times $\overline{a}$ + $y \times $\overline{d}$ \times $\overline{b}$ + $z \times $\overline{d}$ \times $\overline{c}$ = = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0, which is impossible, because $\overline{d}^2$>$ 0.

Answer

No, it is not possible.
