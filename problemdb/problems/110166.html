Problem ID 110166

A set of weights has the following properties:

It contains 5 weights, which are all different in weight.

For any two weights, there are two other weights of the same total weight.

What is the smallest number of weights that can be in this set?

Solution

Let A be one of the lightest weights, and B one of the weights with the weight that is the next weight consecutively after A. Obviously, a pair of weights {A, B} can be balanced only by the same pair.

Therefore, there are at least two weights of weight A and B. A pair {A, A} can also only be balanced by the same pair. Therefore, there are at least 4 weights of weight equal to A. For similar principles, there are at least 4 weights of the heaviest weight E and at least two weights of the previous weight D.

In addition, by assumption, there is at least one weight C, which is heavier than A and B and is lighter than D and E. Thus, the total number of weights in the set is no fewer than 4 + 4 + 2 + 2 + 1 = 13.

On the other hand, it is also easy to verify that a set of 13 weights: {1,1,1,1,2,2,3,4,4,5,5,5,5} satisfies the condition of the problem.

Answer

13
