Problem ID 65204

A unit square is divided into $n$ triangles. Prove that one of the triangles can be used to completely cover a square with side length $\frac{1}{n}$.

Solution

For $n=24 and $n=3$ the statement made by the problem can be checked directly, see the diagrams below.

Let $n \geq 4$. The sum of the areas of the $n$ triangles into which the unit square is divided must equal 1, therefore amongst them there will be a triangle with area $S \geq \frac{1}{n}$.

We shall prove that this triangle can be used to cover a square with side $ \frac{1}{n}$.

Let $a$ be the length of the largest side of the chosen triangle, and $h$ the height of the triangle with respect to that side.

Consider a square, one of whose sides lies on side $a$ of the triangle and two of whose other vertices just touch the other two sides of the triangle, see the diagram.

The opposite side of the square defines another triangle which is similar to the first one, with scale factor $h= \frac{h}{a} = \frac{h-x}{h}$ where $x$ is equal to the side length of the square. Re-arranging this gives $x= \frac{ab}{a+h}$

If $a+h \leq 2$ then by virtue of the inequality $S = \frac{ah}{2} \geq \frac{1}{n}$ we get $x \geq \frac{2}{n} \times \frac{1}{a+h} \geq \frac{1}{n}$.

If in fact $a+h > 2$ then $h > 2-a \geq 2- \sqrt{2}$ since $a \leq \sqrt{2} as the side of the triangle cannot exceed the length of the diagonal of the unit square. Furthermore, $a \geq h$. In fact if $b$ is one of the other sides of the triangle, then $ a \geq b \geq h$. Using these inequalities we get:

$$ x= \frac{h}{\frac{h}{a}+1} \geq \frac{h}{2} \geq \frac{2-\sqrt{2}}{2} > \frac{1}{4} \geq \frac{1}{n}$$
