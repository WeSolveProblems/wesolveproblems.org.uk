Problem ID 79402

Prove that the sequence $x_n = sin (n^2)$ does not tend to zero for n that tends to infinity.

Solution

We use the following trigonometric inequality:

$| sin (α - β) | ≤ | sinα | + | sinβ |$.

Let $sin (k^2) \rightarrow 0$. Choose $ε < {\frac {1} {8}} | sin 2 |$ and an N such that $| sin (n^2) | <ε$ for any n$>$ N. Using the trigonometric inequality given above twice, we obtain:

$| sin ((n + 1)^2 - n^2) | = | sin (2n + 1) | ≤ | sin (n + 1)^2 | + | sin (n^ 2) | $<$2ε$,

$| sin ((2n + 3) - (2n + 1)) | = | sin (2) | ≤ | sin (2n + 3) | + | sin (2n + 1) | $<$2ε + 2ε = 4ε$, from which the contradictory inequality follows: $| sin 2 |$ $<$4ε $<$ $4 \times \frac{|sin2|{8}= \frac {1}{2} = ½| sin 2 |$. $($This solution is from the book Halperin, Tolpygo$)$.
