Problem ID 116873

We are given $n+1$ different natural numbers, which are less than $2n$ $(n>1)$. Prove that among them there will always be three numbers, where the sum of two of them is equal to the third.

Solution 1

We will use proof by induction.

Base step: for $n=2$ the chosen numbers must be $1, 2, 3$. In this case $1+2=3$.

Induction step: Let there be $n+2$ natural numbers smaller than $2n+2$. We will consider two cases.

1) The number $2n+1$ is not present amongst the given numbers. Then, out of the numbers smaller than $2n$, at least $n+1$ numbers are chosen. From the inductive assumption there will be three numbers, where the sum of two of them is equal to the third.

2) One of the given numbers is $2n+1$. Divide the numbers $1, 2, ..., 2n$ into $n$ pairs with a sum of $2n+1$ i.e. $ \{1, 2n \}, \{2, 2n-1 \}, ..., \{n, n+1 \}$. Since there are still $n+1$ remaining numbers of those we have been given, at least one of these pairs will be present amongst the numbers given thereby giving us two numbers that sum to give a third out of the numbers we have been given, as required.

Solution 2

Let $a_1$ be the smallest of the numbers given. Consider the set $ \{ a_2, ..., a_n_+1, a_2+a_1, a_3+a_1, ..., a_n_+_1+a_1 \}$. It contains $2n$ numbers, however all of them lie within the range $ \[a_1+1, a_1+2n-1 \]$ which contains $2n-1$ numbers. Hence the set contains two equal numbers, that is $a_i=a_j+a_1$ as required.

Note

Notice that in Solution 2 we have in fact proved an even stronger statement - one of the chosen three numbers can always be the smallest number given.
