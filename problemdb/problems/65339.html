Problem ID 65339

On board the airplane there are 2n passengers, and the airline loaded for them n servings of lunch with chicken and n servings with fish. It is known that a passenger with a probability of 0.5 prefers chicken and with a probability of 0.5 prefers fish. Let's call a passenger dissatisfied if he does not have what he prefers.

a) Find the most likely number of dissatisfied passengers.

b) Find the mathematical expectation of the number of dissatisfied passengers.

c) Find the variance of the number of dissatisfied passengers.

Solution

a) The number of dissatisfied passengers can be any from 0 to n. In the case of n = 1, everything is obvious: there are either no dissatisfied passengers or there is one dissatisfied passenger, where both cases are equally possible. In what follows, we assume that n$>$1.

We introduce a random quantity ξ "the number of dissatisfied passengers". ξ = 0, only if exactly n passengers prefer chicken, and n others prefer fish. We consider the event when "the passenger prefers chicken" a successful one.

Remarks

1. The result of a) is rather unexpected - the mode of distribution of ξ does not depend on n.

2. If there are, say, 400 passengers on board, we should on average expect that of the passengers will be dissatisfied. This does not mean that you can solve all of the problems with dissatisfied passengers, having only 8 spare sets of food of each kind. There will almost certainly be no dissatisfied passengers $($with a probability of more than 0.95$)$, if you take an extra 42 sets - 21 sets of each kind $($you can check this$)$. Recall that we carried out the calculation in a simplified situation. In life, the likelihood of preferring fish and chicken is not the same. If we took this into account, the calculations would be much more cumbersome, and we would not have had such beautiful results.
